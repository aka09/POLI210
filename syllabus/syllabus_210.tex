\hypertarget{what-is-this-course-for}{%
\section{What is this course for?}\label{what-is-this-course-for}}

This course is an introduction to research methods in political science.
It will cover a broad range of topics, including questions of philosophy
of science; causality and the difficult enterprise of causal inference;
how to ask empirical questions about the political world (and which ones
to ask!); formulating empirically testable hypotheses; measurement and
data collection using surveys and interviews; and qualitative and
quantitative data analysis techniques designed to test your hypotheses.

\hypertarget{pre-requisites-formal-and-informal}{%
\subsection{Pre-requisites -- formal and
informal}\label{pre-requisites-formal-and-informal}}

Formal: There are no pre-requisites for enrolling in this course.
However, if you have already taken POLI311, you are not eligible to take
this course. For those who matriculated in the fall of 2017 or later,
POLI210 serves as a prerequisite for POLI311.

Informal: You do \emph{not} need to have a math/stats background to
succeed in this course. We will provide you with the tools to succeed
regardless of your background.

\hypertarget{learning-outcomes}{%
\subsection{Learning outcomes}\label{learning-outcomes}}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Become critical consumers of political science research and empirical
  claims in popular media.
\item
  Acquire knowledge of important quantitative and qualitative empirical
  methods in the study of politics.
\item
  Become acquainted with \emph{R} -- a free, open-source software that
  is highly marketable (this very syllabus and all course slides were
  created using \emph{R}!)
\end{itemize}

\hypertarget{textbook-and-other-resources}{%
\section{Textbook and other
resources}\label{textbook-and-other-resources}}

\hypertarget{textbook}{%
\subsection{Textbook}\label{textbook}}

The textbook for this course will be:

Berdahl, Loleen and Keith Archer. \emph{Explorations: Conducting
Empirical Research in Canadian Political Science} (4th edition). Oxford
University Press.

You can purchase the print version at the McGill bookstore or rent a
copy at \href{redshelf.com}{redshelf}. Note that we will be using the
newer 4th edition.

\hypertarget{other-resources}{%
\subsection{Other resources}\label{other-resources}}

People learn in different ways and this is \emph{especially true} when
it comes to research methods. What works for me may not work for you!
Fortunately, there are a lot of resources you can use:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  StackOverflow is a Q\&A forum for programmers. If you type a question
  related to \emph{R} in Google, chances are that a StackOverflow thread
  will show up!
\item
  \emph{Quantitative Social Science: An Introduction} by Kosuke Imai is
  an excellent textbook that can help you learn \emph{R} and principles
  of statistics.
\item
  \emph{The Fundamentals of Political Science Research} by Kellstedt and
  Whitten is a widely-used textbook.
\end{itemize}

\hypertarget{labs-r-and-rstudio}{%
\section{Labs: R and RStudio}\label{labs-r-and-rstudio}}

In labs, you will be introduced to quantitative data analysis using the
statistical software \emph{R}, a free and open-source program. We will
also be using RStudio, which is a graphical user interface that makes
learning R easier. You can download \emph{R}
\href{http://www.cran.r-project.org}{here}; note that you need to choose
the version that matches your operating system. You can download RStudio
\href{https://www.rstudio.com/products/rstudio/download/}{here}. Try to
do this as soon as possible! You will need \emph{R} to complete most of
the assignments.

Late assignments will be penalized 5 percentage points (out of 100) per

\hypertarget{re-grading}{%
\subsection{Re-grading}\label{re-grading}}

Students who wish to contest a grade for an assignment or exam must do
so in writing (by email, sent to the instructor) providing the reasoning
behind their challenge to the grade received within two weeks of the day
on which the assignments are returned. The TA who graded the assignment
will re-grade your assignment, and may raise or lower the grade. If you
are still unsatisfied after the re-assessment, you can re-submit the
assignment to me (original copy with TA comments), along with your
justification. I will then re-evaluate the assignment, but also reserve
the right to raise or lower the grade. Please also see
\href{http://www.mcgill.ca/politicalscience/files/politicalscience/assessment_and_\%20re-read_policy_final.pdf}{the
department's guidelines}.

    measures of central tendency, recoding variables, describing
    distributions.
  \item
    Assignment 3, due on October 25th at 11:59pm, will ask you to work
    with experimental data from a famous political science article. It
    will address issues of causality.
  \item

    analyzing open-ended responses from our class survey as well as the
    case-study approach.
  \item

    significance tests, interpreting p-values, and interpreting measures
    of association.

\hypertarget{class-schedule}{%
\section{Class schedule}\label{class-schedule}}

\textbf{Week 1}: Intro

\setlength{\leftskip}{1cm}

\begin{itemize}
\item
  Lecture 1.1 (Sept 2nd): Reviewing the syllabus and course components.
  Differentiating between two types of social scientific research
  (normative vs empirical). Three types of inference in empirical
  research (causal, descriptive, predictive).
\item
  Readings: None.
\end{itemize}

\setlength{\leftskip}{0cm}

\textbf{Week 2}: The foundations of scientific research

\setlength{\leftskip}{1cm}

\begin{itemize}
\item
  Lecture 2.1 (Sept 7th): We will cover some foundational questions:
  What makes science scientific? What are the principles of scientific
  research?
\item
  Lecture 2.2 (Sept 9th): The steps in the scientific process.
  Quantitative and qualitative approaches. How a scientist behaves.
\item
  Readings:

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    \emph{Explorations} chapters 1-2.
  \item
    \href{https://fivethirtyeight.com/features/the-supreme-court-is-allergic-to-math/}{The
    Supreme Court is Allergic to Math}, FiveThirtyEight.
  \item
    \href{https://statmodeling.stat.columbia.edu/2021/03/12/the-social-sciences-are-useless-so-why-do-we-study-them-heres-a-good-reason/}{The
    social sciences are useless. So why do we study them?}, by Andrew
    Gelman.
  \item
    Noel, Hans. 2010. ``Ten Things Political Scientists Know That You
    Don't.'' The Forum 8(3).
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\setlength{\leftskip}{0cm}

\textbf{Week 3}: Building theory and making hypotheses

\setlength{\leftskip}{1cm}

\begin{itemize}
\item
  Lecture 3.1 (Sept 14th): What is a scientific ``literature''? How do
  we come up with a research question, and what makes for a good one?
\item
  Lecture 3.2 (Sept 16th): How to build theory: inductive vs deductive
  approaches. Formulating hypotheses. Hypothesis testing and refining
  theory.
\item
  Readings:

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    \emph{Explorations} chapters 2 and 4.
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\setlength{\leftskip}{0cm}

\textbf{Week 4}: Causality

\setlength{\leftskip}{1cm}

\begin{itemize}
\item
  Lecture 4.1 (Sept 21st): Introduction to the potential outcomes
  framework and the fundamental problem of causal inference. Thinking
  counterfactually. Causal vs correlational relationships.
\item
  Lecture 4.2 (Sept 23rd): How random assignment solves the problem of
  self-selection. Experimental methods in political science. Internal vs
  external validity. The search for quasi-experiments.
\item
  Readings:

  \begin{itemize}
  \item
    Druckman, James N., Donald P. Greene, James H. Kuklinski, and Arthur
    Lupia. 2011. ``Experiments.'' In \emph{Cambridge Handbook of
    Experimental Political Science}, Cambridge: Cambridge University
    Press, 15--26.
  \item
    Butler, Daniel M., and David E. Broockman. 2011. ``Do Politicians
    Racially Discriminate Against Constituents? A Field Experiment on
    State Legislators.'' \emph{American Journal of Political Science}
    55(3): 463--77.
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\setlength{\leftskip}{0cm}

\textbf{Week 5}: Conceptualization and measurement

\setlength{\leftskip}{1cm}

\begin{itemize}
\item
  Lecture 5.1 (Sept 28th): Why do we even need concepts? How do define
  concepts? How do we differentiate one concept from another? The ladder
  of generality and ``definitional gerrymandering''
\item
  Lecture 5.2 (Sept 30th): 4 levels of measurement. Measurement
  obstacles: random and non-random measurement error; social
  desirability bias. Measurement validity.
\item
  Readings:

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    Collier, David, and Steven Levitsky. 1997. ``Democracy with
    Adjectives: Conceptual Innovation in Comparative Research.''
    \emph{World Politics} 49(3): 430--51.
  \item
    Mansbridge, Jane. 2003. ``Rethinking Representation.''
    \emph{American Political Science Review} 97(4): 515--28.
  \item
    Measurement Validity by Collier?
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\setlength{\leftskip}{0cm}

\textbf{Week 6}: Case studies and the comparative method

\setlength{\leftskip}{1cm}

\begin{itemize}
\item
  Lecture 6.1 (Oct 5th): Two types of case studies: descriptive vs
  theory-testing. Why focus on a single case? Scope conditions and
  generalization to other cases. What sort of evidence do we use?
\item
  Lecture 6.2 (Oct 7th): Goals of the comparative method. How to select
  cases: purposive sampling. Mill's method of similarity and method of
  difference. Can we establish causal relations?
\item
  Readings:

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    \emph{Explorations} chapter 10.
  \item
    Geddes, Barbara. 1990. ``How the Cases You Choose Affect the Answers
    You Get: Selection Bias in Comparative Politics.'' Political
    Analysis 2: 131--50.
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\setlength{\leftskip}{0cm}

\textbf{Week 7}: Interviews and focus groups

\setlength{\leftskip}{1cm}

\begin{itemize}
\item
  Lecture 7.1 (Oct 15th): Guest lecture.
\item
  Readings:

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    \emph{Explorations} chapter 7.
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\setlength{\leftskip}{0cm}

\textbf{Week 8}: Midterm + survey research

\setlength{\leftskip}{1cm}

\begin{itemize}
\item
  Lecture 8.1 (Oct 19th): MIDTERM EXAM.
\item
  Lecture 8.2 (Oct 21st): How do we sample for polls? The promises and
  pitfalls of survey research. What happened in 2016 and 2020 -- are
  polls broken?
\item
  Readings:

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    Berinsky, Adam J. 2017. ``Measuring Public Opinion with Surveys.''
    Annual Review of Political Science 20: 309--29.
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\setlength{\leftskip}{0cm}

\textbf{Week 9}: Descriptive statistics and the basics of statistical
inference

\setlength{\leftskip}{1cm}

\begin{itemize}
\item
  Lecture 9.1 (Oct 26th): Measures of central tendency and measures of

\item

\end{itemize}
